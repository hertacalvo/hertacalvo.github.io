Welcome!

Congratulations on your upcoming graduation! This is a great achievement and you should be proud of yourself. I hope you're all excited to celebrate your graduation, to start a new job or graduate school, perhaps to move to a new city, and to start your post-college life.

However, it's okay if life after college feels a little scary too. As with anything in life, post-grad life is likely not all sunshine and happy moments. So, I hope this guide helps you cope with some of the struggles of life after college, even if it's just a little.

Feeling lonely or bored?

It's surprisingly common, but it doesn't have to be (though it's okay if you feel this way). There don't seem to be any statistics on post-college loneliness or boredom, but it's the topic of coutnless publications from sources ranging from Buzzfeed listicles to The Guardian columns.

I've read through a handful of these listicles, columns and editorials to gather these tips on keeping yourself busy, healthy and in touch with old and new friends.

Hobbies

If had any hobbies during or before college, such as recreational sports, gaming or knitting, it's undeniably harder to keep up with them after graduation.

Many places have facebook groups and discord servers where local adults share organize and share info about hobby-related events in your area. Your local subreddit is a great place to ask about these!

Work life

If you're a traditional student, college is a place where all your peers are around your same age. However, this will most likely not be the case when you join the workforce or attend graudate school.

Many workplaces have Employee Resource Groups for recent college grads or otherwise young professionals (ex. NextGen), which are a great way to get to know other young folks in the office.

New Friends

Making after college can be tough, but technology is here to help you. Mainly, you can use tech to engage with your new co-workers and people who share your same hobbies (see above).

However, if you're extroverted, you might consider websites such as meetup.com or friend finder apps such as Bumble BFF (everyone) or Hey! Vina (women only).

Old friends

It's likely that your friend group from college will end up scattered accross the country (or even the world!) after graduation, so keeping in touch with them will be harder when you don't spend every single moment together.

Some good ways to keep in touch are to talk to friends outside the groupchat from time to time, schedule regular phone calls, or even send eachother actual mail.

Mental health

A study showed that most students who experienced mental health issues during college were likely to still experience them 2 years later, which could be after graduation.

Your boredom or loneliness could stem from your mental health, so don't be scared to seek help (your new health insurance might even cover it).

Faith

If you practiced your faith with a local group during college, it can be tough to leave that community behind when you graduate (especially move away).

If you miss being involved with young-people groups you may be too old for, consider becoming a mentor or leader for youth in your community.

Worried about "money stuff"?

After graduation, you will be entering "real adulthood". This might mean more bills and responbilities than you were usde to during undergrad.

If you've gone into a full-time position, you'll probably be earning more more money than ever before. If you've gone on to grad school, you might be facing more bills than ever before, but without that high an income due to being a full-time student.

I've read through a few articles and found these pieces of advice.

Understand your expenses

When you leave on-campus housing and the college lifestyle, your expenses will inevitably creep up.

From housing costs, to health insurance and utility bills, this US News & World Report article make sure to take into account your new expenses when planning your budget.

Start an emergency fund

With a hightened cost of living, emergencies can rise in cost as well. So, it's a good idea to start saving money to avoid going into debt when covering these unexpected expenses.

The Emergency Fund Calculator can help you estimate a how much your should save based on your income and expenses.

Slash your subscriptions

When you're no longer elegible for student discounts, the cost of your substcibtions will definitely rise.

So, this is a good time for you to re-evaluate the subscriptions you actually use and KonMari those you don't.

Check out your local library

A good way to get affordable entertaining is through your local library. Apart from borrowing books, some libraries also offer access to ebooks and streaming services.

You can see more free resources offered by public libraries on this article.

Get cooking

Eating out everyday can get pretty expensive, so cooking at home is a good way to cut costs.

This Forbes article estimates that cooking at home is five times less expensive that getting delivery! Even when using meal kits, frozen meals or simple recipes (like overnight oats), you can still save money by eating at home.

Use your benefits

Many workplaces will offer ample benefits for employees, especially when working in the engineering or technology industry.

Apart from benefits such as health insurance, employers may also offer student loan repayment plans, discounts on local vendors and subsidized public transit passes.

What about the small things?

You can find everythin on campus, from printing services to legal services. So, it's a good thing to keep in mind the above tips before making decisions such purchasing a printer (instead of printing at a FedEx center), or getting a CD adapter for your computer (instead of using the computer at your local library).
